Huston Wilhite Exercise 3

Background: Mean Free Path

The mean free path of a particle traveling through a medium with many scattering opportunities (eg. a photon traveling through opaque material, having many interactions) can be expressed as \[\ell = \frac{1}{n\sigma}\] where n is the number density of scattering particles, and σ is the average effective cross section of those scattering particles (what the cross-sectional area would be if the scattering particles only interacted via hard-sphere scattering rather than ranged forces to get the same effect).

This makes some intuitive sense, since higer number densities (more particles per unit volume) makes it increasingly likely a photon will bounce into one of them over a shorter distance. Similarly, if those scatterers have a larger effective cross sectional area, then again, our photon will bounce into them much more frequently.

1-D Random Walks

The path a particle takes through a series of scatterings is called a "random walk", since we usually can't model exactly how it will move in detail. Instead, we take a statistical approach.

For now, let's assume our photon has only two choices: move backwards, and move forwards. Furthermore, let's assume that it has a uniform mean free path, \(\ell\). Then we have only two choices for each "step": \(\mathbf{d} = \pm\ell\,\hat{i}\). Then after a number N steps, the net displacement will be \[\mathbf{D}(N) = \mathbf{d}_1+\mathbf{d}_2+\cdots+\mathbf{d}_N\] Each individual \(\mathbf{d}_i\) isn't known, but we can make informed assumptions to learn about the typical behavior of random walks in 1-D.

Prompt

  1. Show, assuming 1-D random walk behavior, that the total distance traveled by a photon as it escapes a star of radius R is given by \[L\approx\frac{R^2}{\ell_{\rm photon}}\] In this scenario, the total distance since the beginning of the random walk is R, while the total distance traveled (the number of steps N multiplied by the mean free path) is L. Hint: Use a dot product to find the magnitude squared of the total displacement after N steps as a starting point.
  2. Since photons travel at the speed of light, c, give an estimate for the amount of time the photons take to escape from the center of a star, \(\tau_{\rm phot}\), in terms of the star's radius R and the mean free path \(\ell\).
  3. Read Wikipedia's page on Thomson Scattering (don't worry about being able to reproduce the main results, but get a general understanding of what it is). While many scattering processes exist for photons inside a star, Thomson scattering is an important one. Assume it is the only scattering process active in stars. Furthermore, assume stars have a composition of X=0.7 and Y=0.3 (negligible metallicity). Use these assumptions to calculate an estimate for the mean free path of a star in terms of \(M/M_\odot\) and \(R/R_\odot\). That is, your answer should be in the form of \[\ell = (\textrm{some distance})\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^a\left(\frac{R}{R_\odot}\right)^b\] for some powers a and b.
  4. Use the approximate mean free path from part 3 and the photon escape timescale formula from part 2 to estimate the timescale for photons to travel from a stellar core to its surface, again in terms of \(M/M_\odot\) and \(R/R_\odot\).
